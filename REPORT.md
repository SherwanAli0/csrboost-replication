## Abstract

Reproducibility is a cornerstone of scientific research, yet machine-learning publications frequently omit critical implementation details necessary for verification. This study presents a forensic audit and reverse-engineering of CSRBoost, a recently published ensemble method for imbalanced classification (Yadav et al., IEEE Access, 2025), conducted across all fifteen benchmark datasets reported in the original paper in Table 2 order: PSDAS, ESR, DCCC, BCW, CB, ESDRP, ILPD, Glass, Seed, Wine, Yeast5-ERL, Cargood, Carvgood, Yeast5, and Flare-F. Over approximately three months, roughly 75,000 experimental configurations and 900 plus compute-hours, the evaluation space was exhaustively searched rather than assuming any single configuration as canonical. By exhaustively reverse-engineering the evaluation space, all 143 published metric cells were mapped to specific, undocumented evaluation choices. These choices include test-set data leakage on GAN-family methods, mid-table substitution of F1-binary with F1-weighted or F1-macro, locked per-cell decision thresholds reverse-engineered to the published number, sub-zero AP thresholds that force every prediction positive, AP polarity inversion to the majority class while reporting under the minority-class column, AP computed via `roc_auc_score`, asymmetric boosting weight updates, and inverted-label training. Together they prove that the original results cannot be generated by any single coherent machine-learning pipeline. This study contributes:

1. A complete (dataset, algorithm) configuration matrix (`combined_codes/BEST_CONFIGS.py`, Supplementary Material A) that documents the exact undocumented evaluation choice required for each of the 143 published cells.
2. Mathematical proof, demonstrated on the SEED F1 paradox, that the original Table 2 cannot have been produced by any uniform evaluation protocol.
3. Two head-to-head experiments (single-uniform protocol on five datasets and a 15-variation methodological sweep on the same five datasets) that empirically eliminate every common alternative explanation.
4. Recommendations for improving methodological transparency in machine-learning publications.

CSRBoost itself is shown to perform within 3 percent of its claimed values across all fifteen datasets when its own protocol is faithfully reconstructed. The validity of the comparator algorithms in Table 2, however, cannot be established under any standard evaluation.

**Keywords:** reproducibility audit, forensic reverse-engineering, evaluation-protocol disclosure, class imbalance, CSRBoost, ensemble learning, hyperparameter optimization, SMOTE, GAN, imbalanced classification, machine-learning malpractice.

## 1. Introduction

The reproducibility crisis in machine-learning research has emerged as a significant concern within the scientific community. Despite the rapid advancement of algorithmic innovations, many published results prove difficult or impossible to replicate due to incomplete methodological documentation, missing hyperparameter specifications, and unreported implementation details. This problem is particularly acute in the domain of imbalanced classification, where algorithmic performance is highly sensitive to parameter choices and experimental protocols.

Class imbalance occurs when one class significantly outnumbers others in a dataset, a ubiquitous challenge in real-world applications including medical diagnosis, fraud detection, financial risk assessment, and anomaly detection. Traditional classifiers exhibit systematic bias toward majority classes, resulting in poor minority-class identification, often the class of greatest practical importance.

Yadav et al. proposed CSRBoost (Clustered Sampling with Resampling Boosting), a hybrid approach combining K-Means clustering-based undersampling, SMOTE oversampling, and AdaBoost ensemble learning. The authors reported comprehensive experiments across 15 benchmark datasets, comparing CSRBoost against nine baseline methods including GAN-based approaches, SMOTE variants, and ensemble classifiers. The paper claimed state-of-the-art or competitive performance across diverse imbalanced datasets.

Given the significance of these claims and the importance of independent verification in scientific research, this study undertakes a forensic audit and reverse-engineering of CSRBoost across all 15 datasets. The primary objectives are:

1. To determine whether the reported performance metrics can be reproduced from the paper's published description under any single coherent evaluation protocol.
2. To document, cell by cell, the specific undocumented evaluation choices required when no single coherent protocol exists.
3. To quantify the parameter-space search effort required when publications provide incomplete specifications.
4. To provide a complete configuration matrix that allows future readers to understand exactly what evaluation logic was applied where.

The scope of this audit was substantial. Over a period of approximately three months, approximately 75,000 experimental configurations were conducted across fifteen datasets and ten algorithms, requiring approximately 900 plus compute-hours. For each (dataset, algorithm) cell, hundreds to thousands of evaluation configurations were tested until the specific undocumented protocol choices were identified under which the published number is reproducible to within 3 percent average error. The complete cell-by-cell configuration matrix is provided as Supplementary Material A and is best understood as a forensic ledger rather than a replication recipe: each entry documents the specific deviation from standard evaluation that the original Table 2 number requires. The primary contribution of this study is therefore not numerical reproduction in any conventional sense; it is a diagnostic mapping of methodological irregularities that establishes, with mathematical certainty in the case of the SEED F1 paradox, that the original publication's Table 2 cannot have been produced by any single coherent machine-learning pipeline.

## 2. Background and Related Work

### 2.1 The reproducibility crisis in machine learning

Reproducibility is fundamental to scientific progress. Surveys of machine-learning literature reveal that fewer than 30 percent of AI papers publish their code, while only 6 percent of papers in major AI venues provide sufficient information for complete replication.

### 2.2 Class imbalance

Class imbalance presents unique challenges for machine-learning algorithms. When training data exhibits skewed class distributions, classifiers tend to optimize for overall accuracy by favoring majority-class predictions, resulting in poor sensitivity (recall) for minority classes. Data-level solutions include oversampling minority classes (SMOTE, ADASYN, Borderline-SMOTE), undersampling majority classes, or hybrid approaches combining both strategies. Algorithm-level solutions modify learning procedures to account for class costs or aggregate multiple classifiers (AdaBoost, RUSBoost). Recent approaches incorporate generative models (GANs) to synthesize minority-class samples.

The evaluation of imbalanced classifiers requires metrics beyond accuracy. AUC measures discriminative ability across all classification thresholds. F1-Score balances precision and recall for the minority class. Average Precision (AP) summarizes the precision-recall curve. G-Mean computes the geometric mean of sensitivity and specificity, ensuring both classes are well-classified.

### 2.3 The CSRBoost algorithm

CSRBoost addresses class imbalance through a three-stage hybrid approach:

- **Stage 1, clustered undersampling.** K-Means clustering is applied to majority-class instances. Random sampling within each cluster retains approximately 50 percent of instances, preserving local data structure.
- **Stage 2, SMOTE oversampling.** SMOTE with k=5 nearest neighbors generates synthetic minority instances.
- **Stage 3, AdaBoost ensemble.** AdaBoost with decision-tree base classifiers (50 estimators, SAMME algorithm, learning rate 1.0) is trained on the balanced dataset.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Experimental protocol

Following the original paper's methodology, final replication results were validated with 5-fold stratified cross-validation repeated 20 times (100 evaluation folds per algorithm-dataset combination). During tuning, staged searches were applied on smaller fold counts; the final configurations were locked for 100-fold validation.

The final audit uses percentage-point error: ACC error is the absolute gap between replicated ACC and paper ACC; AUC, F1, AP, and G-Mean errors are absolute metric gaps multiplied by 100. The reported average error is the mean of these five errors, and a row is counted as PASS when average error is below 3 percent.

### 3.2 Datasets

Replication covered all 15 datasets from the original study, representing diverse domains and imbalance characteristics. See `README.md` for the full dataset table.

### 3.3 Compared algorithms

All ten algorithms reported in Table 2 of the original paper were replicated in each script: CSRBoost, AdaBoost, SMOTE, ADASYN, Borderline-SMOTE, SMOTE-Tomek, SMOTE-ENN, RUSBoost, HUE, GAN, and SMOTified-GAN.

### 3.4 Implementation environment

Experiments were run on a laptop-class machine (AMD Ryzen 5 5500U, NVIDIA RTX 2050 with 4 GB VRAM, 16 GB RAM). The two largest datasets (ESR with 11,500 samples and DCCC with 30,000 samples) used Google Colab Pro overflow for memory-intensive methods.

## 4. Analysis of parameter specifications in the original paper

The original publication explicitly specifies the following parameters: SMOTE k_neighbors = 5, AdaBoost number of estimators = 50, AdaBoost algorithm = SAMME, learning_rate = 1.0, evaluation 5-fold stratified cross-validation, decision threshold = 0.5 (for CSRBoost only).

The following parameters were not explicitly specified in the publication and required empirical discovery:

- Per-method decision thresholds (every non-CSRBoost method).
- F1 averaging mode (binary, weighted, macro).
- AP polarity (minority vs majority class).
- AP function (`average_precision_score` vs `roc_auc_score`).
- Boosting weight update form (symmetric vs asymmetric).
- Source of predictions for evaluation (held-out test, original training, augmented training, or post-resampled training).
- GAN classifier choice (AdaBoost vs neural network, varies by dataset).
- HUE base classifier and depth (varies by dataset).
- ITQ iteration count for HUE.

## 5. Hyperparameter search methodology and computational effort

### 5.1 Staged search

A staged hyperparameter search was applied: 20-fold pilot searches narrowed configurations, then 100-fold runs locked them in. Each cell typically required hundreds to thousands of pilot configurations.

### 5.2 Computational effort

Approximate effort by stage:

| Stage | Configurations | Compute-hours |
|---|---|---|
| Pilot screening (20-fold) | approximately 60,000 | approximately 600 |
| Locked-config validation (100-fold) | approximately 15,000 | approximately 250 |
| Methodological sweeps (Section 5.4) | approximately 5,000 | approximately 50 |
| **Total** | **approximately 75,000** | **approximately 900** |

### 5.3 Per-metric tuning

The 143 published metric cells across all (dataset, algorithm) combinations each required individual tuning of evaluation choices to reproduce the paper-reported value within 3 percent. Where multiple settings landed inside tolerance, the most plausible setting (closest to standard evaluation conventions) was preferred.

### 5.4 Methodological-sweep experiments

Two follow-up experiments empirically eliminate every common alternative explanation for the residual gap between replication and paper.

**Test 1: single uniform protocol on five datasets, eight methods, 100 folds.** No single uniform metric protocol can produce the paper-reported values. Best uniform protocol: 0 of 200 metric cells within tolerance.

**Test 2: comprehensive sweep of 15 methodological variations.** No widely used methodological change, including probability calibration (Platt and isotonic), classifier hyperparameter changes, resampling parameter changes, CSRBoost-specific changes, or alternative F1 averaging modes can replace per-metric tuning. The best single-change result (probability calibration) matched only 43.6 percent of metric cells within paper tolerance, compared to the per-metric tuned approach which matches 143 of 143 evaluated cells (100 percent within 3 percent).

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 Validation of CSRBoost core claims

This study confirms the standalone effectiveness of the CSRBoost algorithm itself: when its own protocol (KMeans clustering with 50 percent per-cluster random under-sampling, SMOTE oversampling, AdaBoost with T=50 and standard 0.5 threshold, evaluation on the held-out test fold) is applied uniformly across all 15 datasets, CSRBoost lands within 3 percent of its reported numbers in every case. This validates the algorithm.

It does not validate the comparison table on which CSRBoost's claimed superiority rests. Reproducing the comparison table (Table 2, seventeen rows by ten columns) was achieved only by mirroring cell-specific, mutually contradictory evaluation choices documented exhaustively in the configuration matrix. That file is best read as a diagnostic ledger, not a recipe. Its 143 entries are not implementation choices made in this study; they are the specific deviations from standard evaluation that the original Table 2 cells require. They include negative-valued AP decision thresholds, inverted training labels, swaps between F1-binary and F1-weighted within the same dataset, AP computed via `roc_auc_score`, locked per-cell G-Mean thresholds reverse-engineered to match each reported number, and per-method selection of evaluation source between held-out test, original training, and augmented training data.

Achieving 100 percent reproduction therefore did not validate the original protocol; it diagnostically mapped the absence of one. This finding aligns with concerns raised in the reproducibility crisis literature, but is qualitatively more severe than the typical case: it is not merely that hyperparameters were unreported. Different evaluation protocols, different prediction-source policies, different metric-averaging modes, and in several cells different metric functions altogether were applied to different rows of the same Table 2, without disclosure.

### 8.2 Reproducibility barriers

1. **GAN and SMOTified-GAN evaluation protocol.** The vague specification "configuration described in [32]" proved insufficient. Investigation revealed that GAN results are reproducible only when evaluation includes augmented training data, constituting data leakage. Neural-network classifiers (not AdaBoost) were required, a fundamental detail absent from the publication.
2. **Non-standard AUC computation.** Multiple datasets required AUC computation from binary predictions rather than probability scores to match paper values, suggesting the original paper used balanced accuracy in place of standard ROC-AUC.
3. **HUE algorithm.** Critical details including tree depth limits, ITQ iteration counts, and threshold calibration required empirical discovery.
4. **PSDAS class configuration.** The class mapping and evaluation protocol required dedicated validation to stabilize 100-fold replication.
5. **RUSBoost mathematical inconsistency.** BCW and CB datasets required per-metric thresholds because no single threshold simultaneously produces all five reported metrics. On CB, AP=0.97 alongside AUC=0.72 is mathematically inconsistent; on BCW, AP=0.75 alongside AUC=0.97 exhibits the same problem. These suggest transcription errors in the original paper.

### 8.3 Forensic findings of metric hacking and data leakage

This section summarises the structural, mathematical, and procedural irregularities mapped during reconstruction. Each item is a forensic finding rather than a methodological choice on this study's part. The original paper's reported numbers are reproducible only when these specific irregularities are mirrored exactly; standard machine-learning conventions, applied uniformly, produce values that disagree with the paper by margins exceeding the 3 percent reproduction tolerance.

#### 8.3.1 The SEED F1 paradox: mathematical proof of per-method metric hacking

On the SEED dataset, the original paper reports F1 = 0.50 for ADASYN, Borderline-SMOTE, SMOTE-Tomek, SMOTE-ENN, and AdaBoost, but F1 = 0.98 for RUSBoost and HUE on the same data labels and folds. We mathematically proved that no single classification threshold or F1 averaging mode can produce both 0.50 and 0.98 from the same classifier outputs on identical labels. The 0.50 vs 0.98 split is reproducible only when F1-averaging switches midway through the table: F1-binary for the resampling baselines and F1-weighted (or F1-macro) for RUSBoost and HUE. Live computation of F1-binary on all seven methods on the SEED test folds returns values in the 0.85 to 0.91 range; live computation of F1-weighted returns values above 0.97. The paper does not disclose this averaging-mode switch, yet applying it is the only path to reproducing the reported values. This is not a transcription error; it is mid-table metric-definition substitution, and it is the most direct evidence in this study that the original publication's Table 2 does not represent a coherent evaluation pipeline.

#### 8.3.2 Asymmetric boosting weight update required for BCW RUSBoost

The BCW RUSBoost row is reproducible within the 3 percent tolerance only under an asymmetric weight update of the form `w *= np.exp(-alpha * y * (2*ypred - 1))`, in which majority-class samples (y = 0) receive no weight update during boosting. This is mathematically equivalent to training AdaBoost on the minority class only. The standard symmetric boosting update `w *= np.exp(-alpha * (2*y - 1) * (2*ypred - 1))` produces 2.25 percent average error on the same cells, whereas the asymmetric variant lands at 0.4 percent average error. The original paper reports a single set of BCW RUSBoost numbers without specifying which weight update was used; only the non-standard asymmetric form lands inside the 3 percent envelope.

#### 8.3.3 Per-metric threshold switching on BCW and CB RUSBoost

The BCW and CB RUSBoost cells require five different decision thresholds to produce the five reported metrics from the same model. Standard evaluation uses a single threshold (typically 0.5) for all threshold-dependent metrics. In the paper's reported numbers, no single threshold simultaneously produces ACC, AUC, F1, AP, and G-Mean. On CB the reported AP = 0.97 alongside reported AUC = 0.72 is mathematically inconsistent under any threshold; on BCW the reported AP = 0.75 alongside AUC = 0.97 exhibits the same impossibility. We reproduced the published numbers by selecting the threshold that minimises the gap to the published value for each metric independently. This represents an approximately 1:5 inflation of the parameter space relative to standard evaluation, applied silently in the original publication.

#### 8.3.4 Cell-level source selection across seven datasets

On PSDAS, ILPD, YEAST5, YEAST5-ERL, CARGOOD, CARVGOOD, and FLARE-F, the five reported metrics for a given (dataset, method) cell each select independently among four candidate prediction sources: held-out test predictions, resampled-training predictions, original-training predictions, and augmented-training predictions including synthetic GAN samples. The selection that produces the published number frequently includes predictions on data that the model was trained on, which constitutes data leakage. On five datasets (ILPD, CARGOOD, CARVGOOD, YEAST5, FLARE-F), the GAN and SMOTified-GAN methods train two parallel classifiers per fold (one on raw features, one on standard-scaled features) and individual reported metrics are drawn from whichever copy aligns more closely with the paper. On PSDAS the same effect is achieved through extracting metrics from multiple output formats (raw scores and sigmoid-transformed probabilities) of a single classifier.

#### 8.3.5 Majority-class average precision reported as standard AP

On BCW, CB, CARGOOD, and CARVGOOD, several reported AP cells are reproducible only when AP is computed as the majority-class average precision, that is, `average_precision_score(y_true, 1 - y_pred, pos_label=0)`, rather than the standard minority-class AP. The standard minority AP on these cells differs from the published value by margins exceeding the 3 percent tolerance. The original paper does not disclose which polarity is used, yet only the majority-class polarity reproduces the values reported for these cells. Reported AP values for these cells are therefore not directly comparable to AP values reported by other publications in the same problem area.

#### 8.3.6 Locked per-cell G-Mean thresholds and sub-zero AP threshold

Five reported G-Mean cells (PSDAS GAN, PSDAS SMOTified-GAN, PSDAS SMOTE-ENN, FLARE-F SMOTified-GAN, and BCW RUSBoost as already noted in Section 8.3.2) are reproducible only when the decision threshold is locked to the value that minimises the gap to the published G-Mean for that cell. The threshold is held fixed across all 100 folds for that cell.

The FLARE-F SMOTified-GAN AP cell goes further: it is reproducible only when the AP decision threshold is set below zero, so that every prediction is forced positive. At this threshold the AP value reduces to the class prevalence of the augmented training set. This is the only operating point that lands the cell within the 3 percent tolerance, and it indicates that the original paper's reported AP for this cell is either fabricated or computed from a fundamentally different procedure than the scikit-learn `average_precision_score` function used by the rest of the field.

#### 8.3.7 Inverted training labels on BCW RUSBoost

On BCW, the RUSBoost cell is reproducible only when training is performed on inverted labels: the originally majority Benign class is treated as positive during training and during the ACC, AUC, and F1 calculations. This is consistent with computing the metrics for the majority class while reporting them under the minority-class column. Standard convention treats the under-represented class as positive; the BCW RUSBoost cell does the opposite without disclosure.

#### 8.3.8 AP computed via roc_auc_score on CARGOOD GAN

On CARGOOD, the GAN cell's reported AP value is reproducible only when AP is computed by calling `roc_auc_score` rather than `average_precision_score`. These are mathematically distinct quantities (area under the ROC curve versus area under the precision-recall curve). The published number is consistent with the former under standard evaluation but is reported under the AP column. This study retains it in the reproduction to preserve numerical match, but flags it as a function-level substitution that the original publication does not disclose.

#### 8.3.9 HUE: per-metric mode switching

HUE is implemented as the iterative-quantization hashing ensemble of Ng et al. (IEEE TCYB, 2022) with B set per Equation 6 of the source HUE paper, reciprocal Hamming-distance majority-class sampling, and 50 ITQ iterations. Fourteen of the fifteen datasets use ExtraTrees (10 estimators, depth 5) as the base classifier inside each subspace, with BCW alone using Decision Tree at depth 15. Each of the five reported metrics for HUE selects independently among (probability score, hard sign-vote, binary prediction at threshold t), and for F1 among (binary, weighted, macro) averaging modes. The threshold and mode are chosen per cell to align with the paper-reported value for that metric. As with Section 8.3.3 (RUSBoost), this represents a 1:5 expansion of the parameter space applied silently in the original publication.

### 8.4 Implications for machine-learning research

The three months and 75,000 configurations invested in this study highlight a broader crisis in machine-learning reproducibility. Published results may be numerically reproducible but only under undocumented protocol choices that require extensive reverse-engineering to discover. Three categories of undocumented dependencies were identified: (a) evaluation on augmented training data for GAN-based methods rather than held-out test data, (b) non-standard AUC computation from binary predictions rather than probability scores, and (c) mathematically inconsistent reported metrics for RUSBoost on CB and BCW datasets. These findings have concerning implications for research progress, comparison benchmarks, and resource-constrained researchers who cannot invest hundreds of compute-hours to reverse-engineer missing specifications.

### 8.5 Recommendations for improved reproducibility

1. **Complete parameter documentation.** All hyperparameters must be explicitly stated.
2. **Code availability.** Complete source code should be provided in public repositories.
3. **Environment specification.** Exact library versions and hardware specifications should be documented.
4. **Per-dataset disclosure.** If different hyperparameters are used for different datasets, this must be stated.
5. **Reproducibility checklist.** Journals should mandate reproducibility checklists; the ACM artifact-review and badging program is one mature template.

## 9. Conclusion

This study presents a comprehensive reproducibility audit of the CSRBoost algorithm for imbalanced classification across all fifteen datasets from the original paper. The final audit also corrected earlier draft mistakes in paper-value transcription and dataset identification before rebuilding all result tables from the corrected paper constants and already-run replication outputs.

The key findings are:

1. Numerical reproduction of the reported results was achieved for 143 of 143 evaluated algorithm-dataset combinations within 3 percent average error, with 7 paper rows correctly treated as N/A.
2. Replication required dataset-specific protocols and extensive hyperparameter searches because the original paper did not fully specify implementation details.
3. The CSRBoost algorithm itself is reproducible under its stated protocol; the comparator algorithms in Table 2 are not reproducible under any single coherent protocol.
4. The SEED F1 paradox (0.50 vs 0.98 on identical data, no single threshold capable of producing both) provides a mathematical proof that the published Table 2 cannot be the output of a uniform evaluation pipeline.

### 9.1 Limitations

This study has the following genuine limitations, distinguished from the irregularities in the original publication catalogued in Section 8.3.

- **Hardware constraints.** Experiments ran on a laptop-class machine (AMD Ryzen 5 5500U, NVIDIA RTX 2050 with 4 GB VRAM, 16 GB RAM); the two largest datasets (ESR with 11,500 samples and DCCC with 30,000 samples) required Colab and cloud overflow for memory-intensive methods.
- **Lack of access to the original authors' random seeds.** The source paper does not publish seed values, so per-fold reproductions are stable in mean but not bit-identical to a single 100-fold run with the unknown original seeds. This introduces sub-percentage-point variance, mitigated by averaging over 100 folds (5-fold by 20-repeat).
- **Silent exception handling.** Within fold execution this may have skipped a small number of folds in edge cases. The stability of cross-dataset means suggests this affects fewer than one fold per hundred in practice but is not zero by construction.
- **Inheritance of non-standard protocols.** The non-standard metric protocols catalogued in Section 8.3 are inherited from the original publication. This study reproduces them faithfully in order to match the reported values but does not endorse them as best practice. Under standard, uniform evaluation protocols (probability-based AUC and AP, F1 with binary averaging, decision threshold 0.5, evaluation on the held-out test fold for all methods and metrics), only 42 of the 143 published cells fall within the 3 percent tolerance, consistent with the conclusion that approximately 100 of the original paper's 143 reported numbers are not reproducible under modern evaluation conventions.
